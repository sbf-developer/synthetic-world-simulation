# Synthetic model notes

## Time and state

One simulation tick represents one month. The city is a 24 × 14 lattice grouped into 12 districts. Agents carry their own state; the macro indicators are aggregates derived from those states.

The default run uses 620 households, 64 firms and 635 housing units. Randomness is generated by a seeded Mulberry32 generator, so the same seed and policy mix produce the same run.

## Agent mechanisms

### Households

Each household has:

- skill and sector preferences;
- cash and debt;
- employment and wage;
- housing quality, rent, subsidy and arrears;
- income, wellbeing and sentiment.

Households receive net labor income or an unemployment benefit, pay rent and interest, then allocate a consumption budget across sectors. Higher cash balances increase saving; lower skill increases the consumption share. This is a stylized buffer-stock behavior, not a calibrated life-cycle model.

### Firms

Firms operate in food, making, services or technology. Each firm has productivity, a price, a wage offer, employees, vacancies, revenue, cash and profits. Desired headcount responds to sector demand, productivity and active shocks. Firms reprice from the ratio of realized revenue to expected demand, and close when liquidity and profitability deteriorate severely. Replacement firms are seeded after closures to keep the experiment focused on the system rather than population collapse.

### Labor market

Firms post vacancies after adjusting target headcount. Unemployed households are scored using skill, sector preference, proximity and a small seeded tie-breaker. Hires are paid a firm-specific wage constrained by the policy wage floor.

### Housing

Rent is a function of household demand relative to housing supply:

```text
base_rent = clamp(630 × (households / effective_units)^0.78, 370, 1900)
```

The rent burden is the effective rent payment divided by household income. New construction adds units each month. A rent-squeeze shock temporarily reduces effective supply without changing the underlying policy target.

### Government

The government collects labor-income and consumption taxes, pays unemployment benefits and housing subsidies, and includes a public-services spending line. The base rate accrues monthly interest on household debt. Fiscal balance is reported as tax revenue minus modeled spending.

### Trust and wellbeing

Wellbeing combines employment, housing stress, money security and home quality. Trust is a bounded sentiment index that responds to unemployment, inflation, inequality and wellbeing. It is intended as a visible social feedback variable, not a validated survey estimator.

## Validation roadmap

Before presenting Synthetic as a realistic national simulation, add:

1. Data calibration using national accounts, labor-force, household expenditure, rent and firm microdata.
2. Baseline tests for stylized facts: unemployment persistence, Beveridge-curve movement, Engel curves, wage inequality, firm size distribution and price dispersion.
3. Replicated Monte Carlo runs with confidence intervals and variance decomposition.
4. Alternative behavioral specifications so results are not locked to one set of assumptions.
5. Backtesting and out-of-sample validation against historical episodes.
6. Audit logs and a scenario manifest so every chart can be traced to code, parameters, seed and data version.

The useful scientific question is not “does this world look real?” It is “which mechanisms are supported by evidence, which outputs are robust across specifications, and where does the model fail?”
